# Phishing Prevention: Do's and Don'ts

## Employee Security Guidelines

---

## DO's - Security Best Practices

### 1. Verify the Sender's Email Address
**What to Do:**
- Carefully examine the sender's email address
- Look for typos and misspellings
- Check if the domain matches the claimed organization
- Be suspicious of public email domains (@gmail.com, @yahoo.com) claiming to be from companies

**Example:**
```
❌ security@paypa1-secure.com (typosquatting)
✅ service@paypal.com (legitimate)
```

---

### 2. Hover Over Links Before Clicking
**What to Do:**
- Move your mouse over links without clicking
- Check the actual URL in the bottom corner of your browser
- Verify the domain matches the claimed sender
- Be suspicious of URL shorteners (bit.ly, tinyurl)

**Remember:** The visible text of a link can be different from the actual destination!

---

### 3. Check Email Authentication
**What to Do:**
- Look for DKIM, SPF, and DMARC indicators
- Be cautious of emails that fail authentication checks
- Use email header analyzer tools when in doubt

**Tools:**
- Google Admin Toolbox: https://toolbox.googleapps.com/apps/messageheader/
- MXToolbox: https://mxtoolbox.com/EmailHeaders.aspx

---

### 4. Contact Sender Through Trusted Channels
**What to Do:**
- Use known phone numbers (not numbers from the email)
- Visit official websites directly (don't click email links)
- Use company directory for internal contacts
- Verify unusual requests through a second channel

**Example:**
```
❌ Calling the number provided in a suspicious email
✅ Calling the company's official number from their website
```

---

### 5. Report Phishing Emails
**What to Do:**
- Forward suspicious emails to your IT security team
- Report to reportphishing@apwg.org
- Use the "Report phishing" button in your email client
- Report to the impersonated company

**Why It Matters:**
Your report helps protect others and improves spam filters.

---

### 6. Use Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)
**What to Do:**
- Enable MFA on all accounts that support it
- Use authenticator apps instead of SMS when possible
- Keep backup codes in a secure location

**Why It Matters:**
Even if your password is stolen, MFA prevents unauthorized access.

---

### 7. Keep Software Updated
**What to Do:**
- Enable automatic updates for your operating system
- Keep browsers and plugins updated
- Update antivirus and security software regularly

**Why It Matters:**
Updates patch security vulnerabilities that attackers exploit.

---

### 8. Verify Urgent Requests
**What to Do:**
- Take time to verify urgent requests
- Contact the requester through a known channel
- Follow established procedures even for "urgent" requests
- Escalate to management when in doubt

**Remember:**
Legitimate urgent requests can wait for proper verification.

---

### 9. Check for Personalized Greetings
**What to Do:**
- Look for your name in the greeting
- Be suspicious of generic greetings like "Dear Customer"
- Note that some legitimate emails may use generic greetings

**Context Matters:**
Generic greetings alone don't mean an email is phishing, but combined with other red flags, they add to suspicion.

---

### 10. Look for Grammar and Spelling Errors
**What to Do:**
- Read emails carefully before acting
- Note spelling mistakes and grammar errors
- Be suspicious of awkward phrasing
- Watch for inconsistent formatting

**Why It Matters:**
Professional companies proofread their communications. Errors may indicate a phishing attempt.

---

## DON'Ts - Things to Avoid

### 1. Don't Click Links from Unknown Senders
**Why It's Dangerous:**
- Links can lead to phishing websites
- Links can trigger malware downloads
- Links can exploit browser vulnerabilities

**What to Do Instead:**
- Navigate to websites directly by typing the URL
- Use bookmarks for frequently visited sites
- Verify the sender before clicking any links

---

### 2. Don't Enter Credentials from Email Links
**Why It's Dangerous:**
- Fake login pages steal your credentials
- Attackers can access your accounts
- Stolen credentials may be sold on the dark web

**What to Do Instead:**
- Navigate to login pages directly
- Use password managers with auto-fill
- Check for HTTPS and valid certificates

---

### 3. Don't Trust Urgency or Fear Tactics
**Why It's Dangerous:**
- Urgency reduces critical thinking
- Fear pressures you to act without verifying
- Attackers exploit emotional responses

**Common Tactics:**
- "Your account will be suspended"
- "Immediate action required"
- "Final notice"
- "Legal action will be taken"

**What to Do Instead:**
- Take time to verify the request
- Contact the company directly
- Remember: legitimate issues can wait for verification

---

### 4. Don't Reply to Suspicious Emails
**Why It's Dangerous:**
- Confirms your email address is active
- May trigger additional phishing attempts
- Your response may be used against you

**What to Do Instead:**
- Report the email without replying
- Delete the email after reporting
- Block the sender if appropriate

---

### 5. Don't Assume Logos Mean Legitimacy
**Why It's Dangerous:**
- Logos are easy to copy
- Professional-looking emails can still be phishing
- Visual appearance doesn't guarantee authenticity

**What to Do Instead:**
- Verify the sender's email address
- Check links before clicking
- Look for other phishing indicators

---

### 6. Don't Provide Sensitive Information via Email
**Why It's Dangerous:**
- Email is not secure
- Information can be intercepted
- Legitimate companies never ask for sensitive info via email

**Never Send via Email:**
- Passwords or PINs
- Social Security Numbers
- Credit card numbers
- Bank account details
- Security questions/answers

---

### 7. Don't Bypass Security Protocols
**Why It's Dangerous:**
- Protocols exist for a reason
- "Executives" requesting bypasses may be impersonators
- BEC attacks often use this tactic

**What to Do Instead:**
- Follow established procedures
- Verify executive requests through known channels
- Escalate to security team when in doubt

---

### 8. Don't Trust "Too Good To Be True" Offers
**Why It's Dangerous:**
- Free prizes don't exist
- Lottery winnings require buying tickets
- Unsolicited inheritances are scams

**Common Scams:**
- "You've won a gift card!"
- "Free iPhone giveaway"
- "Unexpected inheritance"
- "Get rich quick" schemes

**Remember:**
If it sounds too good to be true, it IS too good to be true.

---

### 9. Don't Open Unexpected Password-Protected Attachments
**Why It's Dangerous:**
- Password protection bypasses antivirus scanning
- May contain malware or ransomware
- Legitimate companies rarely send password-protected files unexpectedly

**What to Do Instead:**
- Verify with the sender before opening
- Use alternative methods to receive sensitive documents
- Scan with antivirus before opening

---

### 10. Don't Scan QR Codes in Unexpected Emails
**Why It's Dangerous:**
- QR codes can lead to phishing sites
- URLs in QR codes are hidden until scanned
- Mobile devices may have fewer security controls

**What to Do Instead:**
- Verify the sender before scanning
- Navigate to websites directly
- Be especially cautious with QR codes in unsolicited emails
